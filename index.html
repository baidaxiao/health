Part I · Feature 2

Uncertainty about efficacy

According to the British Medical Journal:
  • Only about 1/3 of treatments are shown to work or likely to work.
  • Of the 3,000 treatments studied, 50% were ineffective or unproven.

Why this matters

Patients cannot judge if a treatment is good. So they trust the doctor to decide. This creates an agency relationship (Arrow 1963).

Patients cannot judge quality, so they trust the doctor. This is the root of the principal–agent problem.

Part I · Information problems

Moral hazard: demand side

Insurers cannot see how sick you really are. So they cannot be sure you ask for the right amount of care.

The RAND Health Insurance Experiment (1970s)

  • People were randomly given plans with different coinsurance rates.
  • Price elasticity = 0.2 (price up 10% → use 2% less care).
  • Elective care (dental, mental health) more elastic; hospital care less.
  • Using more care because it was cheap did not make people healthier → real moral hazard.
  • Exception: low-income, chronically ill patients got worse with cost-sharing.

The insurer’s response

Deductibles, copays, coinsurance. Make patients share the cost.

But cost-sharing puts financial risk back on patients → the risk–incentive trade-off (Chapter 4).

Part I · Information problems

Moral hazard: supply side

Provider-side moral hazard works through three channels. Press → to focus each one.

1
Health maximization
If patients are fully insured and doctors fully paid, doctors give care past the point where it is worth the cost.
2
Liability pressure
Doctors fear lawsuits, so they do “defensive medicine.” Evidence: real but limited.
3
Income maximization
Doctors trade off patient health vs their own income. Often there is no trade-off: more tests and low-risk procedures help patients AND raise income.
The third channel is the induced demand hypothesis. But testing it is surprisingly hard. We will see why, and how Gruber & Owings (1996) solved it, in Part II.

Part I · Features 4 & 5

Two more market failures

4 · Imperfect competition

  • Prices vary a lot, even in the same city: knee replacement $6,404–$52,503; lower-limb MRI $270–$3,251.
  • Monopoly hospitals charge 12% more than markets with 4+ competitors.
  • Shopping is hard (especially in emergencies), so competition stays weak.

5 · Inconsistent choices

  • People choose insurance badly (welfare loss of 25–33% of out-of-pocket spending).
  • “Behavioral moral hazard”: even at low copays, people skip high-value care (like blood-pressure drugs).

Physician Moral Hazard in Practice

From Part I to Part II

The identification challenge

The root question from Part I: do doctors respond to financial incentives? If so, supply-side moral hazard is real. But testing this is harder than it looks.

Fertility decline is a pure income shock with no substitution effect. That is why the identification is clean.

Gruber & Owings (1996): Contribution

Uses fertility decline as a pure income shock to cleanly identify induced demand, solving the problem that fee changes cannot separate substitution from income effects.

Part II · Background

Why cesarean sections?

An inverse “scissors”: fertility ↓ as c-sections ↑
  • C-section rate: 5.5% (1970) → rose 240%+ by 1982; reached 23.5/100 births by early 1990s.
  • More profitable: $2,053 vs $1,492 (~40% more).
  • Easier: can be scheduled; often faster than long labor.
Three possible causes:
  • New technology (fetal monitoring)
  • Malpractice fear
  • Financial incentives ← our focus
C-sections pay 40% more but have no clear better outcomes. The 240% rise is suspicious; financial incentives may be at work.

Part II · The model (1 / 3)

The model: setup

Doctor’s utility: U = U(Y, I)
Y
= total income
More income = happier, but each extra dollar matters less (UY > 0, UYY < 0). This is diminishing marginal utility.
I
= total inducement = B × i
How much the doctor “pushes” patients toward c-sections. More pushing = more guilt / worse reputation (UI < 0).
a(i)
= fraction of c-sections
More pushing → more c-sections (a′ > 0, a″ = 0). B = number of births; i = pushing per birth.
Budget: Y = YnN + YcC,   C = B·a(i),   N = B(1−a(i)),   with Yc > Yn (c-sections pay more).

Part II · The model (3 / 3)

The model: comparative statics

What happens when the number of births B falls?

    When there are fewer patients, income falls. Because each dollar now matters more, the doctor does more c-sections to make up the loss.
    This result needs UYY < 0 (diminishing marginal utility / risk aversion). If utility were linear, an income shock would not change behavior.

    Part II · Results

    Main result

    A 10% fall in fertility → the c-section rate rises 0.97 percentage points.
    Table 2, Column (3), preferred specification.
    How large is this?
    • A 10% fertility decline ≈ a 5% drop in ob/gyn income.
    • Typical ob/gyn: 168 births/yr. A 1 pp rise = 1.68 more c-sections × $561 ≈ $943/yr.
    • Average ob/gyn income = $194,300 → this is only a 0.5% income rise.
    • → Doctors made up only about 1/10 of the income loss by doing more c-sections.
    The effect is real, but modest. Doctors do respond to money, but they do not push everyone to c-sections.

    Part II · Conclusion

    Conclusion: Gruber & Owings

    1
    Doctors respond to money, but only a little (about 1/10 of the income loss).
    2
    This is waste, not better care. C-sections are riskier, with no clear proof they improve outcomes.
    3
    Decouple pay from technology cost. If c-sections and normal deliveries paid the same, doctors would choose based on what is best for the patient, not what earns more.

    The big picture

    What we learned

    Moral hazard is not just theory. It is real, and it is everywhere in health care.

    Demand side: patients

    People use more care when they are insured (the RAND experiment).

    ↓
    VBID: pay by value, not price
    Cover high-value care like insulin; charge more for low-value care.

    Supply side: doctors

    Doctors do more c-sections when it pays them more (Gruber & Owings).

    ↓
    Decouple pay from technology cost
    If c-sections and normal deliveries paid the same, doctors would choose what is best for the patient.
    How we pay for care shapes the care we get.
    Good payment design is how we fight moral hazard.

    Discussion

    Questions for the room

    How clean is this identification?

    Most causal studies use a sharp shock, like a law that changes on one day. This paper uses a slow trend over 12 years. State and year fixed effects help, but other things also changed. The identification is clever, but not as sharp as a single policy change.

    Can we design one payment system that fixes both sides?

    We saw two types of moral hazard: patients use too much; doctors provide too much. Can we design one mechanism that solves both at the same time? This is still an open question in health economics.

    Which trade-offs matter most?

    Gruber shows every system faces the same trade-offs. No system can fix everything. So which problems should we solve first, and which can we live with?
